The month of the Ifk

AsrNaam Editorial · From the classical sources

Aisha was left behind on a march when she stepped away from the caravan to look for a lost necklace. A young Companion, Safwan ibn al-Muattal, found her and brought her back on his camel. Munafiqun spread the accusation for a full month. Quranic revelation cleared her by name.

In the sixth year of the Hijra, Aisha bint Abi Bakr was traveling with the Prophet ﷺ and his army returning to Medina from the expedition against the Banu al-Mustaliq. She was around twelve years old. As one of the wives of the Prophet ﷺ she was carried on a camel in a curtained litter for privacy, the litter was strapped between two poles and heaved on and off the camel by hired attendants.

On the evening before the last stage of the return march, the army halted near Medina for rest. Aisha stepped away from the camp to relieve herself. On her way back she noticed the string of onyx beads her mother had given her, a modest necklace, was missing. She went back to look for it. She found it after some searching and returned to the halt.

The camp had already broken up. The attendants who carried her litter had lifted it back onto her camel without checking that she was inside. She was small and light. They assumed the weight of the litter meant she was in it. They set off with the army toward Medina, leaving her behind on the plain.

Aisha realised what had happened when she saw the camp empty. She sat down in the place where the caravan had been. Her only chance was that when they discovered her absence, someone would ride back for her. She wrapped herself in her outer garment and waited.

Safwan ibn al-Muattal al-Sulami, a Companion known for sleeping late, had been at the rear of the army. He came upon the empty campsite as the army was already miles ahead. He recognised Aisha, said innaa lillahi wa innaa ilayhi raajiun, and offered no words to her beyond making his camel kneel. He looked away while she mounted. He walked in front, leading the camel by the halter. Neither of them spoke. He brought her into Medina in broad daylight the following morning.

The rumour began the same day. Abdullah ibn Ubayy ibn Salul, the head of the Medinan munafiqun, put it into the streets: what had she been doing alone with a young man? The rumour spread. It was repeated for a full month.

Aisha herself did not learn of the rumour until nearly the end of the month. She had fallen ill on her return and had been in her mother's house. She noticed that the Prophet ﷺ was distant with her when he visited. She had assumed he was preoccupied with something else. When she recovered enough to be out among the women of Medina, one of them let a comment slip, and Aisha realised the whole city had been discussing her for a month.

She went to her father Abu Bakr and mother Umm Ruman and wept for two days and two nights. She could neither eat nor sleep. She said she felt her tears had reached her liver and she was going to die of them.

The Prophet ﷺ visited her at her parents' house. She could see he was under strain. He said the words that had been prescribed for a case like this in Islamic law: if you are innocent, Allah will make it clear. If you have committed a fault, ask forgiveness and Allah will forgive. He was, in effect, asking her whether she needed to confess or to be defended. He did not know.

Aisha did not answer immediately. She waited for one of her parents to answer for her. Neither did. Both were silent. She said, later, that at that moment she was so hurt by their silence, that even they seemed to be uncertain, that she found strength she had not had before.

She said: "By Allah, I know now that you have heard this thing until it has settled in your hearts and you believe it. If I said to you I am innocent, and Allah knows I am innocent, you would not believe me. If I confessed to a thing I did not do, you would believe me. So I will say what the father of Yusuf said: 'Patience is the beautiful thing. And Allah is the One whose help is sought against what you claim.'"

She turned away and lay down. The Prophet ﷺ was still in the house.

What happened next is preserved verbatim in the classical sources because Aisha herself narrated it many times. The Prophet ﷺ was overtaken by the state that came upon him when revelation descended. He sweated. He shivered under his cloak even though the day was cold. Aisha's mother put her hand on Aisha's shoulder to say courage. The Prophet ﷺ came out of the state, sat up, wiped his face, and smiled.

He said: "O Aisha, praise Allah, for Allah has declared your innocence." He recited the ayat that had just come down. Surah An-Nur 24:11 through 20, nine verses. They cleared her by name. They warned the ummah against spreading rumours. They laid down the Quranic law of qadhf, the four-witness rule for adultery accusations. They rebuked the specific believers who had repeated the rumour without checking.

Aisha's mother said to her: "Get up and go to the Messenger of Allah." Aisha said in her later narration: "I will not get up to him. I will praise no one but Allah."

The men who had repeated the rumour, including Hassan ibn Thabit the poet, Mistah ibn Uthatha (a cousin of Abu Bakr whom Abu Bakr had been financially supporting), and Hamna bint Jahsh, received the Islamic penalty for slander, 80 lashes. Abdullah ibn Ubayy ibn Salul, the ringleader, did not receive the physical penalty (as head of the munafiqun he was politically protected) but was cursed in the Quran itself in the same verses.

The classical Muslim jurists took the verses of the Ifk as the founding text of the Islamic law of slander. The verses established that any accusation of illicit sexual conduct requires four eyewitnesses of the act itself. Anything less is punishable slander. This one Quranic ruling has probably prevented more false accusations against Muslim women across fourteen centuries than any other legal norm in the world.

What the story teaches

The Ifk is remembered because it is one of the few cases where Quranic revelation descended to defend, by name, a specific individual against a public accusation. Aisha's answer to the Prophet ﷺ in her mother's house, the words of Yaqub the prophet from Surah Yusuf, taken up in her own weeping voice by a girl who had had no chance to defend herself, is one of the great moments of feminine dignity in Islamic history. She did not beg. She did not plead. She said patience is the beautiful thing and I trust Allah. The vindication came within minutes. The lesson the classical scholars take is: sabr comes first, then Allah acts. Aisha did not know Allah would act. She said sabr anyway. And Allah acted.
