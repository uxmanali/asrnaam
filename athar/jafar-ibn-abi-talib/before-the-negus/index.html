Before the Negus

AsrNaam Editorial · From the classical sources

The Quraysh sent an embassy to the Christian Negus of Abyssinia demanding the extradition of the Muslim refugees. Ja'far ibn Abi Talib stood in the Negus's court, described what Islam had done for the Arabs, and recited the opening of Surah Maryam. The Negus wept and refused.

In the fifth year of the Prophet ﷺ's mission in Mecca, the persecution of the small Muslim community became severe enough that the Prophet ﷺ instructed a group to emigrate to the Christian kingdom of Aksum in Abyssinia, modern Ethiopia, where a just Christian king called al-Najashi (the Negus) ruled. About 80 Muslims made the crossing, led by Ja'far ibn Abi Talib. They were received hospitably.

The Quraysh, alarmed that their persecution was being circumvented, dispatched a formal embassy to Aksum to demand the refugees' extradition. Amr ibn al-As (still a pagan at this point, decades before his conversion) led the embassy. He brought expensive gifts for the Negus and his court. He addressed the throne.

Amr said: "O king, some fools from among us have taken refuge in your country. They have abandoned the religion of their fathers. They do not follow the religion you follow either. They have invented a religion of their own. Our leaders, their own fathers and uncles, have sent us to you to ask you to return them to us."

The Negus was a shrewd ruler. He said: "I will not return them until I have heard from them what they have to say." He summoned Ja'far and the delegation of Muslims.

Ja'far stood in the court of the Negus in the presence of the Christian bishops of the kingdom. He was in his mid-thirties. He had never spoken in a royal court before. He was a refugee.

The Negus asked him: "What is this religion you have invented that you have separated from your people to follow?"

Ja'far's answer is preserved in nearly every classical source on the sirah. He said:

"O king, we were a people of ignorance. We worshipped idols. We ate carrion. We committed abominations. We severed the ties of kinship. We treated the strong among us as if he had a right to devour the weak. We were in this state until Allah sent us a messenger from among ourselves, whose lineage, honesty, trustworthiness, and chastity we knew. He called us to worship Allah alone, to renounce the stones and idols our fathers and we had worshipped. He commanded us to speak truth, to be trustworthy, to keep the ties of kinship, to be good neighbours, to abstain from what is unlawful and from blood. He forbade us from immoralities, false speech, consuming the property of orphans, and slandering chaste women. He commanded us to worship Allah alone and to associate nothing with Him, to establish the prayer, to give the zakat, and to fast. We believed in him and followed him and worshipped Allah with what he commanded. We abstained from what he forbade. Our people then rose against us. They tortured us and tried to make us return to the worship of idols. When they overpowered us and pressed us so, we came out to your country, choosing you above all others, and hoping in your protection, o king."

The Negus listened. He then asked: "Do you have with you any of what he has brought from Allah?"

Ja'far said: "Yes." The Negus asked him to recite. Ja'far chose to recite the opening of Surah Maryam. The choice was pointed. Surah Maryam is the Quran's account of the story of Isa ibn Maryam, Jesus, son of Mary. It is the Quran's most extended treatment of Christian material.

Ja'far recited. He recited from kaf ha ya ain sad. dhikru rahmati rabbika abduhu Zakariyya, Kaf Ha Ya Ain Sad. The remembrance of the mercy of your Lord to His servant Zakariya, through the account of Zakariya's prayer, the birth of Yahya, the story of Maryam, the birth of Isa. He continued until he came to the verse: wa salamun alayhi yawma wulida wa yawma yamutu wa yawma yub'athu hayyan, peace upon him the day he was born, and the day he dies, and the day he is raised alive.

The Negus wept until his beard was wet. His bishops wept until their books were wet. He turned to Amr ibn al-As and said: "By Allah, this and what Jesus brought come from a single lamp." He turned to Ja'far and said: "Go. By Allah, I will not surrender them to you."

Amr made one more attempt the next day. He said to the Negus: "These people say something dreadful about Isa ibn Maryam. Ask them what it is." He knew that if the Muslims said what the Quran said, that Isa was the servant and Messenger of Allah, not the Son of God, the Negus, as a Christian, would be forced to extradite them for heresy.

The Negus summoned Ja'far again the next day. He asked him: "What do you say about Isa ibn Maryam?"

Ja'far did not lie. He said: "He is the servant of Allah, His Messenger, His Spirit, and His Word, which He cast into Maryam, the virgin."

The Negus picked up a small stick from the ground and said: "By Allah, Isa ibn Maryam did not exceed what you have just said by the length of this stick." His bishops around him disagreed openly with their king. The Negus dismissed them and told Ja'far: "Go. You are safe in my country. Whoever curses you will be punished. I would not accept a mountain of gold to see one of you harmed."

Amr ibn al-As returned to Mecca with the gifts he had brought and no refugees. The Muslim community in Aksum stayed for many years. Ja'far himself returned to Medina in 7 AH just as the Prophet ﷺ was taking Khaybar. The Prophet embraced him and said: "I do not know which pleases me more today: the return of Ja'far or the conquest of Khaybar."

What the story teaches

Ja'far's speech before the Negus is remembered as one of the great da'wa speeches of Islamic history because it did what da'wa is supposed to do: it explained the religion to a stranger in terms the stranger could accept. Ja'far did not begin with fiqh. He did not begin with theology. He began with the moral catalogue of what pre-Islamic Arabia had been and what the Prophet ﷺ had made it. He described the change the religion had wrought in a community, from consuming the weak to protecting them, from severing ties of kinship to keeping them, from slander to trust. The Negus, a just Christian king, heard the moral vocabulary and recognised it. The recitation of Surah Maryam was the completion of the argument: the shared theological ground was not a demand but an invitation. This is the classical Islamic model of da'wa to non-Muslims, moral intelligibility first, shared prophetic history second, doctrinal difference last. Ja'far did it once and every Muslim missionary since has been trying to do it again.
