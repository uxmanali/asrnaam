Abd ar-Rahman ibn Awf. A story from Athar

Abd ar-Rahman, and the caravan of seven hundred camels

Wealth as a trust; the merchant who wanted to walk into Paradise, not crawl

The rumor reached Madinah first, then the dust of the caravan, then the caravan itself. Seven hundred camels loaded with cloth, wheat, oil, spices, and household goods from Sham. The lead camel bore the mark of the house of Abd ar-Rahman ibn Awf. The people came out of their houses to watch. Something was happening on the road that was going to shake the streets.

Aisha, wife of the Prophet ﷺ, heard the noise from her chamber. She asked what it was. She was told: a caravan of Abd ar-Rahman has come. Aisha sat back and said: I heard the Messenger of Allah ﷺ say once about Abd ar-Rahman that he would enter Paradise crawling.

The word passed through the streets of Madinah, from house to house, from the market to the palm-groves, until it reached Abd ar-Rahman himself. He was in his house. He turned the phrase over. Entering Paradise crawling meant entering Paradise, and any believer would take that. But Abd ar-Rahman had not become the merchant of the ummah in order to crawl. He got up and went to Aisha's chamber. He knocked on the door. He asked: mother of the believers, did you hear the Messenger of Allah ﷺ say that about me?

Aisha said: yes. I heard it.

He said: then I ask you to bear witness for me. The entire caravan, the seven hundred camels, everything they carry, the saddles and the ropes, all of it, I give as sadaqa in the path of Allah. I will not have it said of me on the Day of Resurrection that I crawled into Paradise while I could have walked.

He turned around. He walked to the caravan drivers. He gave the order. Everything was distributed to the poor of Madinah before nightfall.

That was the practice of a lifetime, not a single moment. Abd ar-Rahman ibn Awf had emigrated from Mecca to Madinah with nothing. His host in Madinah, Sad ibn ar-Rabi of the Ansar, had offered him half of his own house, half of his wealth, and one of his two wives in divorce so Abd ar-Rahman could marry her. Abd ar-Rahman had refused all of it. He had said the words that became famous among the Muhajirun: may Allah bless your family and your wealth for you. Show me the way to the market.

He went to the market of Bani Qaynuqa. He bought and sold cheese and dried curds. Within days he was making money. Within months he was profitable. Within a year he was a merchant. By the time he emigrated he had rebuilt his fortune three times over, first in Madinah, then in the trade routes to Sham, then in the caravans of the conquest period.

He never mistook the source of his wealth. When the Battle of Tabuk came and the Prophet ﷺ called for donations, Abd ar-Rahman gave two thousand gold dinars. It was half of his total wealth at that moment. The Prophet ﷺ said to him: may Allah bless what you have given and what you have kept.

Aisha said in her old age: I saw Abd ar-Rahman ibn Awf, and I never saw a man like him. When his caravans arrived and the earth shook with the sound of them, it was because his generosity was not less than his profits.

When he died, he left in his will four hundred dinars to each surviving veteran of Badr. There were still some living. When Ali received his four hundred, he stood at the graveside and said: rest well, Abd ar-Rahman. You fed us while you lived, and you fed us again from your grave.

What the story teaches. A believer who happens to be wealthy is being tested by both directions at once. The wealth can carry them into Paradise on horseback, or drag them into it crawling, or bar the door. Abd ar-Rahman refused to crawl. He knew wealth was a trust. When the Messenger of Allah ﷺ told the truth about him, he made the truth good: he gave the seven hundred camels in one hour so he would walk into Paradise on his own two feet. The wealth was never his. It was always a trust. The instant he learned it might weigh him down, he set it down.
