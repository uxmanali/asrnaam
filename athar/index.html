Athar

The traces they left. Stories of the men and women whose lives still shape ours.

Athar (آثار) is the plural of athar: trace, footprint, monument, legacy, impact. It shares its Arabic root ا-ث-ر with the name of AsrNaam itself. If AsrNaam is the reading of the impact of your name, Athar is the reading of the impact of theirs.

Each hero here is drawn from the classical Islamic historical sources, from the great sirah literature, from Ibn Ishaq and al-Tabari and al-Waqidi and the works of the classical Muslim historians. The stories are told with what the sources say. We do not romanticise. We do not add. The lives were dramatic enough as they were.

The heroes

c. 592 - 642 CE

خالد بن الوليد Khalid ibn al-Waleed

The undefeated general. Fought against the Muslims at Uhud, embraced Islam six years later, and never lost a single battle across more than a hundred engagements. Died in bed, weeping that he did not fall in battle.
c. 584 - 644 CE

عمر بن الخطاب Umar ibn al-Khattab

The second Rashidun caliph. Set out with a drawn sword to kill the Prophet and returned that same day as a Muslim. Ruled an empire from Egypt to Persia and mended his own sandals. Killed at fajr prayer in the mosque.
c. 601 - 661 CE

علي بن أبي طالب Ali ibn Abi Talib

Cousin and son-in-law of the Prophet. The first male child to embrace Islam. Slept in the Prophet's bed on the night of the Hijra to save his life. Broke the gate of Khaybar. Fourth Rashidun caliph.
1137 - 1193 CE

صلاح الدين الأيوبي Salahuddin al-Ayyubi

The Kurdish sultan who recaptured Jerusalem for Islam eighty-eight years after the First Crusade. When he entered the city in 1187, he forbade a massacre and let the Christian inhabitants ransom themselves. Died with nothing.
d. c. 634 CE

نسيبة بنت كعب Nusaybah bint Ka'ab

Went to Uhud with a waterskin to give the wounded water. When the Muslims broke and the Prophet was exposed to enemy blades, she took up a sword, tied her waist, and stood over him with her body as his shield. Received thirteen wounds that day.
c. 573 - 634 CE

أبو بكر الصديق Abu Bakr al-Siddiq

First adult male to accept Islam. Sole companion of the Prophet in the cave of Thawr on the night of the Hijra. First Rashidun caliph. Held the fracturing ummah together after the Prophet's death.
d. c. 720 CE

طارق بن زياد Tariq ibn Ziyad

A Berber Muslim commander who crossed the strait from Africa to Iberia in 711 CE with seven thousand men. Faced a Visigothic army of a hundred thousand. Won. Andalusia was Muslim for the next seven and a half centuries.

Why we call it Athar

In classical Arabic ethics, al-athar al-baqiyah, "the enduring trace", is the technical term for what a person leaves behind that keeps affecting the world after they are gone. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said: "When a person dies, all their deeds come to an end except three: a running charity, useful knowledge left behind, or a righteous child who prays for them." Each of these is an athar: a footprint, a legacy that keeps its work going long after its owner is buried.

The people you'll read about here left athar. Their fingerprints are still on the world we walk on. A Muslim child today reciting the Quran owes something to Uthman's mushaf, which owes something to the twelve hundred huffaz who died at Yamama, which owes something to Khalid ibn al-Waleed. A modern Muslim woman standing up in public owes something to Nusaybah bint Ka'ab. A modern Muslim political theorist arguing that no ruler is above the law owes something to Umar and the Egyptian boy. This is what Athar is here to preserve.
