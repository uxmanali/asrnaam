The day he burned the boats

AsrNaam Editorial · From the classical sources

In 711 CE a Berber Muslim commander named Tariq ibn Ziyad crossed the Strait of Gibraltar with seven thousand men. He landed at the great rock at the tip of Iberia. He ordered his ships burned. He said to his men: the sea is behind you, the enemy is ahead of you. Choose.

In the eighth year of the second century of Islam (711 CE), the Umayyad governor of Ifriqiya, Musa ibn Nusayr, was approached by an emissary. The emissary was Julian, count of Ceuta, a Christian Byzantine ruler who had turned against the Visigothic king Roderic of Iberia. Julian offered ships and information. He wanted the Muslims to invade Iberia and remove Roderic. Musa, cautious, sent a small reconnaissance force in 710 that returned with encouraging reports. In 711 he sent a full expedition under the command of Tariq ibn Ziyad.

Tariq was a Berber freedman of Musa. His men were mostly Berber converts. The force was about seven thousand strong. They crossed the strait, a narrow passage of twelve miles between two continents, and landed at the great rock at the southern tip of Iberia. Tariq made his camp at its foot. The Arabs called the rock, from that day, Jabal Tariq: "Tariq's mountain." That name was inherited by the Spanish as Gibraltar and by every European language after them.

Roderic, meanwhile, was in the north of his kingdom fighting Basque rebels. When the news reached him that a Muslim army had landed at Gibraltar, he turned his own army south. It was a Visigothic host reported at somewhere between forty thousand and a hundred thousand men. The disparity in numbers between Roderic's army and Tariq's was so total that the Muslim commanders debated whether to retreat back across the strait.

According to a persistent tradition, Tariq gathered his men on the beach in front of their landing site. Behind them was the Strait of Gibraltar. Ahead was the road into Iberia and, somewhere ahead of that road, Roderic's army coming south. Tariq ordered his ships burned.

He said to his men: "The sea is behind you. The enemy is ahead of you. Where would you retreat to?" And he burned the ships.

The classical Arab historians preserve the speech Tariq is said to have given his men that morning. Whether the exact words are historical or reconstructed after the fact, the spirit of them is the spirit of what the Berber-Muslim army did next: "O people, whither would you flee? The sea is behind you and the enemy is before you. Nothing remains for you but the truth of your religion and the strength of your arms. Know that you are in this island more forsaken than the orphans on the tables of the miserly. Your enemy comes against you with his host, his weapons, his numbers, and his abundance of provisions, while you have no support but your own swords and no provisions but what you can wrest from the hands of your enemy."

He led his army north. They met Roderic's army at the Guadalete river in July 711. The battle was brief and decisive. The Visigothic centre collapsed. Roderic himself disappeared during the battle; his horse and crown were found in the marshes; his body was never identified. The Visigothic kingdom was destroyed in a single afternoon.

Tariq did not wait. He pushed north into Iberia. Cordoba fell within weeks. Toledo, the Visigothic capital, fell within months. Musa ibn Nusayr crossed with reinforcements the following year and continued the conquest. By 715 CE, most of the Iberian Peninsula was under Muslim rule. The Muslim frontier eventually pushed as far as the foothills of the Pyrenees, and by 732 raiding parties had reached the outskirts of Tours in France before Charles Martel checked them.

Tariq did not become wealthy from the conquest. Musa ibn Nusayr, jealous of his subordinate, filed complaints against him at the Umayyad court. Tariq was recalled to Damascus and lived out his remaining years there in modest circumstances. He died around 720 CE. His rock kept his name.

What Tariq did in 711 CE lasted seven and a half centuries. The Muslim presence in Iberia, Al-Andalus, produced Cordoba as one of the great cities of the medieval world, produced Ibn Rushd and Ibn Arabi and Ibn Bajjah and Ibn Tufail, produced Maimonides in the Jewish tradition, produced the Alhambra of Granada, and passed to Christian Europe the whole of Greek philosophy through Arabic translation. The intellectual road from ancient Athens to modern Paris passed through the libraries of Al-Andalus. All of it began with a Berber freedman on a beach at the foot of a rock, telling his men that the ships were burning behind them.

What the story teaches

The story of Tariq at Gibraltar is remembered by Muslims for the moment on the beach when he burned the ships. It is one of the great scenes of decisive commitment in the Islamic historical imagination. There is no going back. The road ahead is the only road. What Tariq did that day worked because he understood what an army of seven thousand men, weighed against a hundred thousand, would think about all night if it had the option of the sea behind it. He removed the option. He forced his men to be at their sharpest by removing every path except forward. It is a story used in Islamic pedagogy to teach a specific virtue: the burning of retreat as a precondition of arrival. Whatever it is you intend to do, to memorise the Quran, to marry your beloved, to fight your enemy, to accept Islam itself, the story of Tariq says: burn the boats behind you. Do not leave yourself the road home.
