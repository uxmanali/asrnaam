The al-Buni Elemental Framework: Reading the 28 Letters as Fire, Earth, Air, and Water

Asrnaam Editorial · May 2026

Ahmad ibn Ali al-Buni divided the Arabic alphabet into the four classical elements. Here is how the framework works, what it claims, and what it means when several elements meet in a single name.

The figure

Ahmad ibn Ali al-Buni (d. ~622 AH / ~1225 CE; the precise date is not firmly established in the classical sources and several candidates exist) was a North African scholar of letters who lived and worked in Cairo in the late Ayyubid period. He was a near-contemporary of Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi (d. 638 AH / 1240 CE), though the two worked in different keys. Where Ibn Arabi developed a sustained metaphysical reading of the Arabic letters as ontological principles, al-Buni produced an applied, classified, and at times talismanic system — the most ambitious single body of letter-science to come down from the medieval Islamic world.

Al-Buni's reputation rests primarily on one book: Shams al-Ma'ārif al-Kubrā — "The Great Sun of Knowledge." It is a long, encyclopaedic compilation that gathers letter-symbolism, divine-name groupings, planetary correspondences, numerical (abjad) calculations, and — in its later sections — a great deal of material that classical scholars would describe as al-sīmiyā' or al-rūhāniyyāt: a craft of inscribed talismans, magic squares, and operative invocations. The book has been transmitted, copied, and consulted continuously since the 13th century. It has also been continuously contested.

The contested status of Shams al-Ma'arif

This contestation matters and we will not bury it. The most influential criticism is Ibn Khaldun (d. 808 AH / 1406 CE), who in the Muqaddima treats the operative and talismanic sections of Shams al-Ma'ārif as standing outside the boundaries of orthodox Sunni discipline. Many mainstream scholars after him have followed Ibn Khaldun's lead — separating the symbolic and contemplative use of letters, which can sit comfortably within the broader Sufi tradition, from the operative and talismanic use, which sits closer to what mainstream Sunni scholarship has long considered impermissible.

This article concerns only the first register. The al-Buni material that survives in the working Ilm ul Huroof tradition — the elemental classification of the twenty-eight letters, the per-letter symbolism, the linking of letters to divine names — is read here as a contemplative schema, a way of structuring how a reader meets a name. It is not used for operative or talismanic purposes, and we make no claim about the permissibility of those other practices.

A clear boundary The elemental schema below is presented as the Ilm ul Huroof tradition's reading of al-Buni, not as mainstream Sunni doctrine. Where the elemental cast and a more widely-accepted Arabic-lexical or theological reading of a name diverge, the lexical and theological reading anchors. The element layer is offered as one register among several, not as the final word.

The classical four elements in Islamic cosmology

Before the elements are mapped onto letters, a short note on what they meant in classical Islamic thought. The four elements — al-'anāsir al-arba'ah — entered Arabic philosophy from the Greek inheritance, especially Aristotle and Galen, by way of the 9th-century Arabic translation movement in Baghdad. Al-Kindi (d. ~256 AH / 873 CE), al-Farabi (d. 339 AH / 950 CE), and Ibn Sina (d. 428 AH / 1037 CE) all received and transformed the schema. By the time it reaches al-Buni it has been a commonplace of Islamic philosophy for three centuries.

The four elements are not "elements" in the modern chemical sense. They are principles of being. Each combines two of four primary qualities: hot, cold, dry, wet.

  • Fire — hot and dry. The principle of ascent, transformation, ignition, illumination.
  • Earth — cold and dry. The principle of form, stability, foundation, containment.
  • Air — hot and wet. The principle of motion, connection, breath, transmission.
  • Water — cold and wet. The principle of receptivity, flow, descent into depth, mercy.

In classical Islamic medicine these qualities cluster into the four humours and the four temperaments (al-amzijah) used to describe human disposition. The same vocabulary travels through Ibn Sina's al-Qānūn fī al-Tibb ("The Canon of Medicine"), the philosophical treatises, and the Sufi commentaries. When al-Buni assigns elements to letters, he is working within a shared classical vocabulary, not inventing one.

The four groups

Al-Buni distributes the twenty-eight Arabic letters into four groups of seven. The arithmetic is exact: 7 × 4 = 28. Within each group, the seven letters share an elemental character but carry distinct symbolic registers of it.

Fire (نارية · nāriyyah)

The seven letters of fire are: Alif, Hā', Ṭā', Mīm, Fā', Shīn, Dhāl — ا ه ط م ف ش ذ.

Fire is hot and dry. In the elemental cast these are the letters of ignition and ascent — the principles that begin, illuminate, and transform. Alif stands first: origin, uprightness, the vertical principle that initiates. Hā' (the light ه) is divine presence and the breath that closes the Name of Allah. Ṭā' is the heavy emphatic — purity, weight, the letter of the ṭayyib (the wholesome). Mīm is the maternal-merciful principle — classed here with fire because in this tradition mercy is generative, not passive: it transforms what it touches. Fā' opens — fatḥ, the opening, the disclosure. Shīn is the radiant, the spreading, what diffuses outward from a centre. Dhāl is the letter of remembrance — dhikr — the principle by which the unseen is brought into present awareness.

See the Asrnaam pages for Alif, Hā' (light), Ṭā' (heavy), Mīm, Fā', and Shīn. (The letter Dhāl does not yet have a dedicated page on Asrnaam.)

Earth (ترابية · turābiyyah)

The seven letters of earth: Bā', Wāw, Yā', Nūn, Ṣād, Tā', Ḍād — ب و ي ن ص ت ض.

Earth is cold and dry. These are the letters of form, foundation, and stable containment — the principles that hold. Bā' is the vessel, the container, the foundation — and the first letter of the Bismillāh that opens every surah but one. Wāw is the connector, the linking, and — the principle that binds. Yā' is address and intimacy ("O") but in elemental terms it is also the principle of receptivity-in-form. Nūn is interiority, the hidden fish, the dark depth that nourishes. Ṣād is sincerity and integrity — the form held without breach. Tā' (the light ت) is the principle of resolution into form, the letter that closes many feminine forms through tā' marbūṭah. Ḍād — the letter sometimes called the distinguishing letter of Arabic itself — is the principle of pressed-down strength, what is held firmly in place.

See Bā', Wāw, Yā', Nūn, Ṣād, Tā' (light), and Ḍād.

Air (هوائية · hawā'iyyah)

The seven letters of air: Jīm, Zāy, Kāf, Sīn, Qāf, Thā', Ẓā' — ج ز ك س ق ث ظ.

Air is hot and wet. These are the letters of breath, motion, and transmission — the principles that carry. Jīm is gathering — jam' — the principle of bringing together. Zāy is ornament, beauty, what adorns. Kāf is sufficiency, the divine kāf of kun ("Be") — the letter that commands. Sīn is secret and hidden depth — and the breath that carries the secret outward. Qāf stands as the cosmic mountain, the standing thing, the letter of elevated stability; al-Buni's schema reads it under air as the carrier of weight rather than the weight itself. Thā' is what is broken open or distributed. Ẓā' is appearance, what is visible from the outside, the surface that reveals.

See Jīm, Zāy, Kāf, Sīn, Qāf, Thā', and Ẓā'.

Water (مائية · mā'iyyah)

The seven letters of water: Dāl, Ḥā', Lām, 'Ayn, Rā', Khā', Ghayn — د ح ل ع ر خ غ.

Water is cold and wet. These are the letters of flow, mercy, and descent into depth — the principles that receive. Dāl is guidance — the pointing finger, dalīl. Ḥā' (the heavy ح) is the breath of life, the divine exhalation. Lām is divine binding, the letter that anchors the Name of Allah and stands twice in Allāh. 'Ayn is the spring, the eye, the source — the principle of fresh water and of seeing. Rā' is mercy and return — raḥmah and rujū'. Khā' is hiddenness, khufyah — what is withdrawn from sight. Ghayn is the cloud, the veil, the obscured.

See Dāl, Ḥā' (soft), Lām, 'Ayn, Rā', Khā', and Ghayn.

Why the symmetry matters

Seven letters per element, four elements, twenty-eight total. This arithmetic was not invented to fit the schema — it is the structure of the Arabic alphabet itself, and al-Buni's classification draws attention to it. Ibn Arabi makes the same arithmetic central in a different register: the twenty-eight letters correspond, in his presentation, to twenty-eight divine names, twenty-eight lunar mansions (manāzil al-qamar), and twenty-eight stations of unveiling on the spiritual path.

For the elemental schema the symmetry has a particular consequence: every name, regardless of length, is composed from letters drawn from these four groups. A name of three letters samples three elemental registers; a longer name samples more; some names concentrate in a single element while others span all four. This is the foundation of what we will call the elemental cast of a name in the next section.

The four elements are not equal in their action. In the classical schema, Fire and Air are the active elements (hot), Earth and Water the passive (cold). Fire and Earth share dryness; Air and Water share wetness. A name dominated by Fire is read differently from one dominated by Water; a name with both active and passive elements reads differently again. The element layer adds vocabulary, not substitutions.

The temperaments and the names

When a name is read through the elemental schema, the basic move is to ask which elements appear, where they appear, and in what proportion. A name of three Fire letters has a nāri temperament — quick to ignite, ascending, bright, prone to burn what it touches. A name of three Water letters has a mā'i temperament — receptive, deep, slow to anger, prone to fold inward. A name mixing Air and Water carries motion and depth — a kind of carrying current — while a name mixing Fire and Earth carries ignition and form, a particular structural intensity.

The temperaments are not predictions. A nāri name does not produce a hot-tempered person. It describes a register of meaning the name carries — a climate, in the older sense of the word. The bearer of the name can inhabit that climate, push against it, or live mostly outside it. The reading is contemplative; the life is the bearer's.

Three layers, not one Asrnaam reads every name through three layers held side by side: root etymology (the Arabic three-letter root and its lexical sense), dictionary meaning (the word the name is, as classical lexicons give it), and the Ilm ul Huroof reading (the letter-by-letter and elemental structure). Where the layers reinforce each other, the reading is strong. Where they conflict in core meaning, the mainstream Arabic and Islamic scholarly position anchors — the IUH reading sits on the consensus, not against it. The elemental schema is one register within the third layer. It deepens a reading; it does not override the first two.

What happens when elements mix in a name

Most names contain letters from two or three elements. The reading then becomes a question of constellation: which element opens the name, which closes it, which dominates the interior, and where the tensions sit between them. Two short examples, kept brief here — the full method is the subject of a forthcoming companion article on the parental constellation method.

Yusuf (يوسف)

Letters: Yā' (Earth) · Wāw (Earth) · Sīn (Air) · Fā' (Fire). See the Yusuf reading.

The cast is Earth-Earth-Air-Fire. The name opens with a doubled Earth — Yā' followed by Wāw — and the opening posture is solidly grounded: form, foundation, receptivity held in stable structure. The interior introduces Air with Sīn — breath, motion, the carrier of secret. The name resolves in Fire with Fā' — opening, disclosure, transformation.

A reading might then synthesise: Yusuf begins in grounded receptivity (Earth doubled), breathes through hidden motion (Air at the heart), and resolves in opening and disclosure (Fire at the close). The historical Yusuf — the Prophet عليه السلام whose long Quranic narrative is the story of a name grounded by suffering, carried through hidden providence, and resolved in the opening of recognition — is a notable convergence between the elemental cast and the lexical-historical reading. Both layers describe the same shape. The tradition reads this kind of convergence as a sign of the framework's coherence, not as evidence that the letters predicted the life — the cast describes a shape; the bearer fills it.

Maryam (مريم)

Letters: Mīm (Fire) · Rā' (Water) · Yā' (Earth) · Mīm (Fire). See the Maryam reading.

The cast is Fire-Water-Earth-Fire. The name opens with Mīm — the maternal-merciful principle in its Fire register — and closes with Mīm again, framing the name in its strongest element. The interior holds Water (Rā', mercy and motion) and Earth (Yā', receptive form).

A reading might synthesise: Maryam is framed in mercy that burns — Mīm at both ends — with the interior carrying flowing compassion (Water) held in receptive form (Earth). The classical lexicons place the name within registers of elevation and devoted servanthood; the historical Maryam عليها السلام is the only woman named in the Qur'an, an entire surah given her name — a figure carried by mercy and held in form. The elemental cast and the lexical-historical reading point at the same shape.

"Four elements, twenty-eight letters, one alphabet, many names. Each name is a small constellation drawn from this larger map."

In neither example is the elemental cast doing the whole work. It is sitting alongside the lexicon, alongside the historical bearer, alongside the per-letter symbolism, contributing one register among several. That is the discipline of the framework.

What the framework asks of the reader

A short list, in the same spirit as the earlier articles. The elemental schema asks four things of the reader who wishes to use it well.

First, hold it lightly. The cast describes a climate, not a destiny. No bearer of a Fire-heavy name is condemned to burn what they touch. Names describe shape; lives fill the shape with content.

Second, hold it alongside the other two layers. The root etymology and the dictionary meaning come first. The elemental cast deepens the reading they begin; it does not override them. Where they conflict, the mainstream lexical reading anchors.

Third, attend to position. Just as the per-letter reading weighs the first and last letters, the elemental cast weighs the element that opens and the element that closes. These two carry more weight than the interior elements.

Fourth, beware of single-element names. A name composed entirely of Fire letters reads as concentrated nāri — luminous, fast, intense, and prone to consume. A name composed entirely of Water reads as concentrated mā'i — receptive, deep, slow, and prone to fold inward. Single-element names are not wrong; they are particularly single-keyed, and the bearer of such a name often spends a lifetime learning the other three keys their name does not contain.
