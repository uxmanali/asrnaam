Why Your Name's Urdu Spelling Matters: The Twenty-Eight Arabic Letters Beneath Both Scripts

Asrnaam Editorial · May 2026

Most readers come to Asrnaam through an Urdu-spelled name, an English transliteration, or both. The Arabic alphabet sits beneath all of them, and the Ilm ul Huroof reading travels intact across the script you happen to write your name in.

Two scripts, one tradition

Urdu uses the Arabic script, but adds letters that classical Arabic does not have: the retroflex consonants ٹ, ڈ, ڑ; the nasal ں; the aspirate ھ; the closed-shape ے; and the four Persian additions پ چ ژ گ. These letters entered the alphabet centuries after the script's origin, added by Persian and then South Asian writers to render sounds the classical Arabic ear did not need to distinguish.

Most Arabic-origin Muslim names, the kind we are interested in here, enter Urdu through Persian and arrive with their classical Arabic letters intact. The twenty-eight letters of the Arabic alphabet sit inside Urdu's larger letter set. When you write Muhammad as محمد in Urdu, every letter is one of those twenty-eight. The same goes for یوسف, مریم, فاطمہ, ابراہیم, and the rest. The Ilm ul Huroof reading applies wherever an Arabic-28 letter appears, in any script, in any country.

Some parents who grew up writing only Urdu wonder whether their child's name, spelled in Urdu, is somehow "less authentic" than the Arabic. The structural answer is short: the letters are the same letters. The spelling is the community's signature, not a different name.

Why the same name has different English spellings

The transliteration problem is older than the internet. The same Arabic name, say عائشة, has been spelled in Roman letters as Aisha, Aysha, Ayesha, Ayisha, 'Ā'ishah, and a half-dozen further variations depending on the convention the writer was raised in.

These conventions are not arbitrary. Aisha is the most common Anglicised popular form. Ayesha is the South-Asian Hunterian form preserved across Pakistani and Indian English. Ayisha tracks how a Karachi schoolteacher might transliterate the Urdu عائشہ. 'Ā'ishah is the ALA-LC academic form with diacritics. None of these is "wrong." They are different conventions for rendering an Arabic-script word into Roman letters.

The Arabic letters underneath are identical. So is the Ilm ul Huroof reading. The English spelling is a window on the same room.

The spelling-cluster examples

A short tour of the priority names you are most likely to be looking up: Arabic spelling, Urdu spelling, common English variants:

  • Muhammad, Arabic محمد / Urdu محمد, Muhammad, Mohammad, Mohammed, Muhammed
  • Aisha, Arabic عائشة / Urdu عائشہ, Aisha, Ayesha, Aysha, Ayisha
  • Fatima, Arabic فاطمة / Urdu فاطمہ, Fatima, Fatimah, Fathima
  • Maryam, Arabic مريم / Urdu مریم, Maryam, Mariam, Mary (the biblical cognate)
  • Yusuf, Arabic يوسف / Urdu یوسف, Yusuf, Yousuf, Yousef, Joseph
  • Ibrahim, Arabic إبراهيم / Urdu ابراہیم, Ibrahim, Ebrahim, Abraham
  • Musa, Arabic موسى / Urdu موسیٰ, Musa, Moosa, Moses
  • Omar, Arabic عمر / Urdu عمر, Omar, Umar, Omer
  • Hassan / Hussain, Arabic حسن / حسين / Urdu حسن / حسین, Hassan/Hasan, Hussain/Hussein/Husain
  • Abdullah, Arabic عبد الله / Urdu عبد اللہ, Abdullah, Abdallah, Abdulla

The biblical cognates, Joseph, Abraham, Mary, Moses, are not transliterations of the modern Arabic. They are the same name carried through Hebrew, Greek, and Latin into English over two and a half millennia. The root is shared. The shape has weathered the transmission. The Ilm ul Huroof reading of the Arabic-script original still describes the figure the English-speaking Christian or Jew calls Joseph, Abraham, Mary, or Moses.

How Urdu inherited the Arabic letter readings

The Ilm ul Huroof tradition reads the twenty-eight letters of the classical Arabic alphabet. Al-Buni's elemental schema covers exactly those twenty-eight. Ibn Arabi's distribution of the al-Asmā' al-Husnā covers exactly those twenty-eight. The tradition is rooted in a closed alphabet, and that closure is a feature, not a limit, it means the readings have been refined and contested across a thousand years on a fixed set.

The seven or eight letters that Urdu adds: the retroflexes ٹ ڈ ڑ, the nasal ں, the aspirate ھ, the closed ے, the Persian-Urdu پ چ ژ گ, are linguistically Urdu's own innovation. They render sounds the classical Arabic ear did not distinguish. The classical Ilm ul Huroof literature does not treat them, because they did not exist when the literature was written. Honest scholarship says so plainly: the tradition reads the twenty-eight; the Urdu-only letters are outside its scope. This is not a gap in the tradition. It is the tradition holding to its own foundations.

In practice this rarely matters for names. The overwhelming majority of Muslim names in Urdu are Arabic in origin and use only the twenty-eight Arabic letters. Look at the names a Pakistani family typically chooses: Muhammad, Aisha, Yusuf, Maryam, Fatima, Ibrahim, Ali, Hassan, Hussain, Ahmad, Zainab, Abdullah, Hamza, Khalid, Omar. Every letter is an Arabic-28 letter. Every Ilm ul Huroof reading applies directly.

The Urdu seal: ہ vs ة

The feminine seal in two scripts Arabic feminine names: Aisha, Fatima, Maryam, Khadija, end in tā' marbūṭah (ة), a letter shaped like a closed hā' with two dots above. Urdu writes the same closing sound as chashm-i ha (ہ), an open hā' without the dots. The two characters look different on the page. They sound the same. And: for Ilm ul Huroof purposes, they are the same letter: the underlying letter is Tā', the principle of resolution into form, the feminine seal. The Urdu orthography preserves the meaning; only the glyph differs. If your name ends in ہ in Urdu and ة in Arabic, you carry the same closing principle in either script.

A practical guide: read your Urdu-spelled name through Ilm ul Huroof

Four short steps to apply the same method you would use for an Arabic name to a name written in Urdu.

Step 1. Write your name in Urdu. If you also have the Arabic, write both side by side and confirm the letters match (allowing for ہ/ة and other orthographic variations).

Step 2. Identify each letter. Flag any retroflex (ٹ ڈ ڑ), nasal ں, aspirate ھ, closed ے, or Persian-Urdu addition (پ چ ژ گ), these are Urdu's own innovations and the Ilm ul Huroof tradition does not read them. For most Arabic-origin names, none of these will appear.

Step 3. For each of the twenty-eight Arabic letters in the name, look up the meaning on the Asrnaam letters reference. Note the element (Fire, Earth, Air, Water) from al-Buni's framework and the primary symbolic register.

Step 4. Apply the same first-letter / interior / last-letter reading described in How to Read a Name. The structure of the reading is identical to what you would build from the Arabic original.

A worked example: reading "Ayesha"

Take the English spelling Ayesha, the South-Asian Hunterian form a Pakistani family typically uses on a birth certificate. Write it in Urdu: عائشہ. Write it in Arabic: عائشة. The letters are the same: 'Ayn, Alif, hamza, Shīn, Tā' (whether sealed as ہ or ة).

Three layers, not one Asrnaam reads every name through three layers held side by side: root etymology (the Arabic three-letter root and its lexical sense), dictionary meaning (the word the name is, as classical lexicons give it), and the Ilm ul Huroof reading (the letter-by-letter and elemental structure). Where the layers reinforce each other, the reading is strong. Where they conflict in core meaning, the mainstream Arabic and Islamic scholarly position anchors, the IUH reading sits on the consensus, not against it.

The Ilm ul Huroof reading: 'Ayn opens (the spring, the eye, the source, Water in the elemental cast). Alif and Shīn carry the interior (origin and radiance, both Fire). Tā' marbūṭah resolves (Earth, the feminine seal of form). Water opening into Fire sustained, resolving in Earth. The reading is the same whether your mother wrote Ayesha in Lahore, Aisha in Cairo, or Ayisha in Toronto.

"The letters do not move when you change the script you write them in."
