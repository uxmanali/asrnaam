Abu Dharr al-Ghifari. A story from Aathaar

Abu Dharr, and the shahada he shouted in the Kaaba

The truth said out loud; the first man to accept the risk of open declaration

Abu Dharr al-Ghifari, whose given name was Jundub ibn Junadah, was of the Bani Ghifar. His tribe camped on the caravan road between Mecca and Sham. They were highwaymen. They robbed pilgrims. They had no time for the affairs of Quraysh. But Abu Dharr, alone among them, had walked away from idolatry years before he ever heard of Muhammad ﷺ. He had told his tribe: I will not worship a stone that cannot see me or hear me. His tribe considered him strange.

Then the word reached Ghifar. Someone had appeared in Mecca claiming to be a prophet. Abu Dharr sent his brother Unays, who was a poet and a good judge of speech, to investigate. Unays returned days later and said: I have seen a man in Mecca who commands good and forbids evil, and whose speech is neither poetry nor soothsaying, but I could not verify what he is. Abu Dharr said: you have not satisfied me.

He set out for Mecca himself. It was a dangerous journey. If Quraysh knew who he was seeking he would be killed on sight. He arrived in the city, went to the sanctuary, and lay down in it, waiting for someone to guide him without revealing himself. He lay there for three days. On the third day Ali came, saw the stranger, and understood at once. Ali took him to his house without asking questions. On the fourth night he took him to the Prophet ﷺ. Abu Dharr entered. The Prophet ﷺ asked him about his tribe. He told him. The Prophet ﷺ recited to him. Abu Dharr embraced Islam then and there. He was, by his own count later, the fifth or sixth person to enter Islam.

The Prophet ﷺ said to him: return to your people. Teach them. Wait for a call from me before you come back.

But Abu Dharr had a stubbornness in him. He said: by the One in whose hand my soul is, I will go out into the courtyard of the Kaaba tomorrow and I will declare my Islam publicly.

The Prophet ﷺ said: they will kill you.

Abu Dharr said: this is a matter of truth, and I have to say it.

The next morning he went to the Kaaba, in the middle of the courtyard where Quraysh were gathered for their morning trade, and he shouted at the top of his voice: I bear witness that there is no god except Allah, and I bear witness that Muhammad is the messenger of Allah.

The Quraysh fell on him. They beat him until they thought they had killed him. Al-Abbas ibn Abdul-Muttalib, the uncle of the Prophet ﷺ, was among them but was not yet a Muslim. He recognized Abu Dharr as being of Ghifar and shouted: leave him. He is of Ghifar. Their tribe controls your road to Sham. If you kill him you will lose your caravan route. They pulled back.

Abu Dharr, bruised and bleeding, went back to his hiding place at Ali's. The next morning, before he set out for home, he went back to the Kaaba, in the same place, and did it again. He shouted the shahada in the courtyard for the second time. He was beaten again. Al-Abbas intervened again. Abu Dharr said: this is not something you can keep me from saying. Then he left Mecca.

He returned to Bani Ghifar. He taught them. Under his teaching, half the tribe accepted Islam. The other half accepted after the emigration. Then the neighboring tribe of Aslam accepted. By the time Abu Dharr rejoined the Prophet ﷺ at Khaybar, he was bringing with him an entire population of new Muslims that he had converted in the deserts of northern Hijaz. The Prophet ﷺ received him with love and gave him a place among the closest Companions.

Years later, in a hadith preserved in Ibn Hanbal, the Prophet ﷺ said to Abu Dharr, foreseeing his end: you will live alone, you will die alone, and you will be resurrected alone. Abu Dharr was exiled to the desert of Rabadha by the Caliph Uthman for his sharp criticism of Umayyad wealth. He died there in his tent. His wife, alone in the desert, had no cloth for his shroud. She waited by the road until a caravan came. In the caravan was Abdullah ibn Masud, who was passing through Rabadha on his way from Kufa. When she told him who lay dead in the tent, Abdullah wept. He said: the Messenger of Allah ﷺ told the truth about you, Abu Dharr. He gave up his own shroud, and Abu Dharr was buried there.

What the story teaches. The first time Abu Dharr entered a public space to declare his Islam, the Prophet ﷺ himself asked him not to. It was too dangerous. Abu Dharr disobeyed the request out of what he described as necessity: this is a matter of truth, and I have to say it. Islam is built by people willing to declare, in public, at cost, what they have believed in private. Abu Dharr's shouted shahada in the Kaaba was not martyrdom, but it was the shape of what martyrdom looks like: a body ready to receive the price of the words. He said the words twice, took the beating twice, and then went home to teach his tribe. The truth said out loud, at real cost, is what turns a private conviction into a public religion.
