Sumayya bint Khayyat. A story from Aathaar

Sumayya, and the first blood of Islam

Endurance without exit; the woman who did not renounce

Sumayya bint Khayyat was old, worn from a lifetime of servitude, and small in stature when Abu Jahl walked toward her in the desert outside Mecca. Her husband Yasir was already dead. He had been tortured to death two days before, alongside her, in the same clearing. Her son Ammar was being tortured a short distance away, and the sound of it reached her.

She was not their equal in any of the categories Mecca measured people by. She was not free. She was not of noble tribe. She was not wealthy. She had no protector. Her enslavement had ended only when Islam entered her heart. She had been one of the first seven human beings to enter Islam, along with her husband and her son. The Prophet ﷺ had spoken of her family with love. He had said, when he passed by the three of them being tortured and knew that he could not intervene: patience, family of Yasir. Your appointment is with Paradise.

Now Abu Jahl was standing over her. He was one of the strongest men in Mecca. He was the leader of the tribe of Bani Makhzum. He was the one Quraysh sent when they wanted a matter finished. He had been trying for days to break Sumayya. He had used every method the pre-Islamic Arabs knew. She had not renounced.

He asked her one more time to renounce Islam. She refused with a word.

He drove a spear into her belly.

The Muslim historians who preserved this scene, from Ibn Hisham forward, kept it in the simplest sentences they could find. There was no long speech. There was no debate. There was no scholar there to record the exchange. There was Abu Jahl and there was Sumayya, and the whole of pre-Islamic Mecca stood behind Abu Jahl with everything it had, and Sumayya was alone with a spear in her belly and Allah in her heart. She died.

Her body was left in the sand.

She became the first martyr of Islam. Before Badr, before Uhud, before any of the men who would later die with the standard in their hand, Sumayya had died. She died without a battle, without a weapon in her own hand, without the dignity of a chosen field. She died the way many later martyrs would die, without recognition in the moment, without safety, and without giving her killers what they wanted.

Ammar was tortured to the edge of death after his mother's murder. To save his life he gave a false renunciation with his tongue. He then came weeping to the Prophet ﷺ, and he said: they did not release me until I abused you and praised their idols. The Prophet ﷺ said: how did you find your heart? Ammar said: at rest in faith. The Prophet ﷺ said: if they do it to you again, do it again. And the ayah came down for this exact case in Surah An-Nahl verse 106: unless it be one who is coerced while his heart is at rest in faith.

Sumayya had not chosen that verse. Sumayya had refused the tongue-renunciation. She had died with the shahada in her mouth. Her son survived and lived to fight at Badr, at Uhud, at Khaybar, at Yamama, at Yarmouk, at Qadisiyya. Ammar became one of the greatest fighters of the first generation of Islam. In every battle he fought he was fighting because his mother had died first.

When he was old and blind, at Siffin under the caliphate of Ali against Muawiya's army, Ammar was killed on the field. When the Muslims recognized his body, some of them wept because they remembered the hadith that the Prophet ﷺ had said thirty years earlier: Ammar, you will be killed by the transgressing party. Ammar's own death confirmed a prophecy the Prophet ﷺ had made long before. And behind that death, at the beginning of everything, was Sumayya, in the sand, refusing.

What the story teaches. The Quran later gave permission to say the words of denial with the tongue if the heart stays firm. Sumayya did not take that permission. She refused with the tongue as well as with the heart. In her, Islam met the extremity of the pre-Islamic order, and Islam did not blink. Her death is what the entire early martyrology of the Muslim community is built on. Before the first battle, before the first flag, before the first city, there was a small woman in the desert who did not renounce.
