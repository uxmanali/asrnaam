Yamama, and the false prophet's garden of death

AsrNaam Editorial · From the classical sources

When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ died, half the tribes of Arabia turned away. In central Arabia, a man named Musaylima called himself a prophet and gathered forty thousand men against Islam. Khalid was sent to meet him. The garden into which the last of Musaylima's men retreated has been called the Garden of Death ever since.

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ died in Medina in Rabi al-Awwal, 11 AH (June 632 CE). Within weeks, tribes across Arabia that had accepted Islam only recently began to renounce it. Some believed the whole religion had died with the Prophet. Some refused only to pay zakat and thought they could keep their prayer. Some had accepted false prophets who now claimed the mantle Muhammad had left. The most dangerous of these was Musaylima ibn Habib in central Arabia, whom the histories call Musaylima al-Kadhab, "Musaylima the Liar."

Musaylima was of the Banu Hanifa. He had actually met the Prophet during a delegation of his tribe to Medina. He returned home to Yamama, invented verses in mock-Quranic cadence, married the false prophetess Sajah, and by 11 AH had gathered under his banner an army reported at forty thousand fighting men. Abu Bakr, the first caliph, was told: this is not a rebellion. This is a war of the Prophet's death.

Abu Bakr sent an initial expedition under Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl, who was defeated. He sent a second under Shurahbil ibn Hasanah, who was defeated. Then he sent Khalid ibn al-Waleed.

Khalid arrived at Yamama with about thirteen thousand men, many of them the elite muhajirun and ansar veterans who had fought at Badr and Uhud. Facing him across the plain of Aqraba was a Banu Hanifa army three times his size, fighting on their own ground, defending their own homes, and led by a man they still believed to be a prophet.

The first day went badly for the Muslims. The Banu Hanifa line held. Muslim positions were broken. Khalid's own tent was overrun and its ropes cut. Musaylima's men reached the Muslim rear. Khalid rode among his men and rallied them personally, calling out the names of the Prophet's companions, telling them that this was the war Allah's Messenger had promised them, that this was the day the ummah would either be preserved or lost. By the second day the Muslim discipline held. By the third day the Banu Hanifa lines broke, and Musaylima and the remnants of his army fell back into a great walled garden of Yamama.

What happened next is remembered even in the pre-modern age as one of the most terrible small-space engagements ever fought. The garden was walled on all sides. Inside were thousands of Musaylima's men in a compressed space. To reach them, the Muslims had to breach the wall. A companion named al-Bara ibn Malik, drunk on nothing but the certainty of martyrdom, asked to be thrown over the wall. His companions lifted him bodily and threw him over. He landed among Musaylima's men, opened the gate from inside, and the Muslim army poured in.

The garden was so choked with the dead that afterward the Arabs called it Hadiqat al-Mawt, the Garden of Death.

Musaylima was killed by Wahshi ibn Harb, the Ethiopian slave who had killed Hamza, the Prophet's uncle, at Uhud years before. Wahshi had accepted Islam after the conquest of Mecca. He said later that he had killed the best of men in his days of jahiliyya, and had now killed the worst of men in his days of Islam, and hoped Allah would forgive the first for the sake of the second. Wahshi's spear did the work at Yamama. Nusaybah bint Ka'ab, wounded and one-handed, fought at Yamama too and helped strike Musaylima down: some accounts credit her with the final blow.

The Muslim losses at Yamama were terrible: over twelve hundred killed, among them a large number of huffaz, the men who had memorised the Quran. It was the shock of that loss, more than anything, that moved Umar to approach Abu Bakr and urge him to collect the scattered fragments of the Quran into a single mushaf before more huffaz died in the wars ahead. That collection became the basis, later, of Uthman's standardised codex.

What the story teaches

Yamama is a battle whose consequences ran further than its immediate victory. The false prophet was killed. The Ridda apostasies collapsed within months. But the deeper harvest was that Yamama forced the ummah to face the mortality of its own memory. The Quran had been carried until then in the chests of men, and men die in battle. Uthman's mushaf, the standardised written Quran that every Muslim reads today, has Yamama as its distant, blood-soaked origin. Every Muslim child reciting from a mushaf owes a small debt to the men who fell in that walled garden.
