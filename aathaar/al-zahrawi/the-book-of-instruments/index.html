Al-Zahrawi. A story from Aathaar

Al-Zahrawi, and the drawings that saved a million lives

Careful documentation of technique becomes the ground of a whole medical tradition

In the year 1000 CE, Abu al-Qasim Khalaf ibn Abbas al-Zahrawi sat in his home in the town of Madinat al-Zahra near Cordoba, capital of Umayyad Andalus, drawing surgical instruments. He drew a scalpel with a curved blade. He drew a pair of forceps with fine tips for removing splinters. He drew a syringe with a plunger for injecting solutions into wounds. He drew a needle with an eye for stitching. He drew a set of tonsillectomy tools that would work by pushing the tonsil up against a slot and cutting cleanly. He drew a retractor for holding the edges of a wound open. He drew each instrument with careful, accurate proportions.

He was writing a book. The book was called Kitab al-Tasrif li-man Ajaza an al-Talif, roughly, The Method of Medicine for Anyone Who Cannot Read the Whole of Medicine. It was going to be an encyclopedia of everything he knew, arranged so that a practicing physician could use it as a reference. Al-Zahrawi was the personal physician to the Caliph al-Hakam II of Andalus. He had trained under the best physicians of Cordoba. Cordoba was, at that moment, the most sophisticated medical city in the Western world. Al-Zahrawi was writing what he had learned so that other physicians who did not have access to Cordoba could still practice good medicine.

The book grew. It became thirty volumes. Twenty-nine of them covered diet, pharmacy, physiology, urology, obstetrics, orthopedics, ophthalmology, dentistry, and general medicine. The thirtieth was on surgery.

The thirtieth book had a difference. Al-Zahrawi filled it with his drawings. He drew every instrument he had used or designed. He drew more than two hundred of them. Beside each drawing he wrote how to use the instrument, when to use it, and what to do if it broke or misfired. He gave step-by-step protocols. He described the operation. He described the position of the patient. He described the pressure to apply. He described what to do if the wound would not close.

He described a cesarean delivery. He described a tracheotomy. He described the extraction of arrows. He described the removal of nasal polyps by inserting a hook and pulling. He described a technique for hemorrhoid removal. He described lithotripsy for kidney stones. He described the treatment of a skull fracture with a graded set of scrapers. He described the use of catgut for internal sutures because catgut dissolves as the body heals. This was his own innovation.

For dentistry, he described the correct positioning of forceps and the extraction of teeth without damaging the surrounding alveolar bone. He described dental prostheses made of ox bone. He described the treatment of dislocated jaws.

For obstetrics, he described the correct position of the patient in a difficult labor, the use of forceps for a dead fetus, and the maternal signs that required urgent intervention.

For ophthalmology, he described the treatment of cataracts by needle aspiration through the sclera. His diagrams show the exact angle. This technique remained the standard cataract surgery for eight hundred years.

For orthopedics, he described the reduction of dislocated joints, the setting of broken bones, and the use of plaster and splints.

He also described what a physician should do if a technique failed. He described the signs of infection. He described what to do when a wound would not stop bleeding. He described the moral responsibility of the surgeon. He wrote: a surgeon must never operate on a wound he does not understand. He must know the anatomy under the skin. He must know when to cut and when to stop.

The Kitab al-Tasrif was completed around 1000 CE. It became the standard medical textbook of Andalus within a generation. It was copied in dozens of manuscript versions across the Islamic west. In the twelfth century, at the great translation school of Toledo, Gerard of Cremona translated the thirtieth book, the Book of Surgery, into Latin. He called it Chirurgia Albucasis.

Chirurgia became the standard surgical textbook of medieval Europe. It was used at Bologna, at Padua, at Montpellier, at Paris. It was still being reprinted in the sixteenth century. It shaped the whole European tradition of surgery. When medieval European surgeons finally began to develop their own techniques, they built on what al-Zahrawi had given them.

The instruments al-Zahrawi designed are still in use. His surgical scissors have not changed. His forceps have not changed. His retractors have not changed. His approach to wound closure and to the stepwise ligation of bleeding vessels is now standard emergency medicine everywhere in the world.

Al-Zahrawi died in Cordoba in 1013 CE, at nearly eighty. His grave was destroyed during the Christian reconquest of Andalusia. But the drawings survived. The book survived. The instruments survived. Every surgeon working anywhere in the world today, from the emergency rooms of Karachi to the operating theaters of Boston, is using tools that were first drawn by a hand in Madinat al-Zahra a thousand years ago.

What the story teaches. A careful drawing of a scalpel, made with the intention that another physician might benefit from it, becomes the ground of a whole medical tradition. Al-Zahrawi did not invent surgery. He documented it, illustrated it, systematized it, and gave it away in a form future generations could actually use. A hundred generations of surgeons have since practiced with tools he first drew. What is preserved with real care is what survives across centuries.
