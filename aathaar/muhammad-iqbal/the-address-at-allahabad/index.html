Muhammad Iqbal. A story from Aathaar

Iqbal, and the address that named a country

A poet finishes a vision by giving it a political form

Sir Muhammad Iqbal stood in front of the annual session of the All-India Muslim League in the city of Allahabad on the twenty-ninth of December 1930. He was fifty-three years old. He was a philosopher, a poet, and a lawyer. He was already famous across the Muslim world for his Persian and Urdu poetry. He had a doctorate from the University of Munich on Persian metaphysics. He had studied under McTaggart at Cambridge. He had translated Rumi into idioms that a modern reader could hear. In his own poetry he had taken the Persian tradition of Hafez, Rumi, and Bidel and turned it toward the twentieth century, addressing the whole of the Muslim world at once.

He was the President of the Muslim League session that year. The League was still a small organization. It represented Muslims of British India who wanted more than the Indian National Congress was willing to give them. The Congress under Gandhi was fighting for Indian self-rule, but it was speaking, in Muslim ears, more and more in a distinctly Hindu idiom. Muslim leaders were beginning to ask whether a Congress-led India after independence would in practice mean a Hindu-led India in which Muslims were a minority under Hindu political majoritarianism.

Iqbal opened his speech. He spoke for two hours in English, the language of the imperial administration. The address was long. It moved through philosophy, history, religion, and constitutional theory. The middle of it contained the sentence that changed the century.

He said: I would like to see the Punjab, North-West Frontier Province, Sind, and Baluchistan amalgamated into a single state. Self-government within the British Empire, or without the British Empire, the formation of a consolidated North-West Indian Muslim state appears to me to be the final destiny of the Muslims, at least of North-West India.

He kept speaking. He argued that Islam in India was a nation, not just a religion. He said that the two national communities of British India, the Hindu majority and the Muslim minority, had different visions of civilization. He said that the assimilation of Muslims into a Hindu-dominated India would either destroy Islam in India or would poison the Indian polity.

He proposed a solution. Constitutional structures that would give Muslim-majority regions substantial political autonomy within a federated India. If that could not be achieved, then a separate Muslim state in the northwest, on the lands where Muslims were already the majority.

The speech did not immediately convince everyone. Muhammad Ali Jinnah, then leading the Muslim League, was at that point still committed to a united India and would not fully accept the two-nation theory for another seven years. Jawaharlal Nehru, from the Congress side, dismissed the idea as impractical. The British government was cautious. But the words had been spoken. The idea had been given a shape and a place.

Over the following years the idea grew. Choudhry Rahmat Ali coined the acronym Pakistan in 1933, from Punjab, Afghania, Kashmir, Sindh, and Baluchistan. Jinnah moved toward the two-nation theory over the course of the 1930s. By 1940, ten years after Iqbal's Allahabad Address, Jinnah delivered the Lahore Resolution demanding a separate Muslim state. In 1947 the demand became a reality.

By then Iqbal had been dead for nine years. He had died in Lahore in April 1938 CE, at the age of sixty-one. The last poem he wrote, hours before his death, ended with the couplet:

*The tune that has passed may or may not come. A breeze from the Hijaz may or may not come. The days of this faqir have come to an end. Another knower of secrets may or may not come.*

But he had left the Address. He had left the Javid Nama, in which he had ascended through the heavens with Rumi as his guide, meeting the Prophet ﷺ, and had returned with the message that Muslim civilization must reclaim its own selfhood, its khudi, and take political and spiritual responsibility for its own future. He had left the Bang-i-Dara, the call of the marching bell, which had summoned the Muslim youth of British India to a renewed self-confidence. He had left the doctoral thesis on Persian metaphysics that had connected the classical Sufi tradition to the modern debate about self, will, and community.

When Pakistan was created in August 1947, Muhammad Ali Jinnah became its Governor-General. Jinnah publicly said that the country was Iqbal's dream and that he, Jinnah, had merely carried it into political existence.

Iqbal is buried in a red sandstone tomb, the Mazar-e-Iqbal, in the courtyard between the Badshahi Mosque and the Lahore Fort. Every Muslim schoolchild in Pakistan today reads his poetry. His Urdu couplets are quoted in political speeches, in wedding songs, and in Friday sermons. The country he named at Allahabad in 1930 is the country he is buried in.

What the story teaches. A poet finishes a vision by giving it a political form. Iqbal had spent his whole life in poetry re-awakening the Muslim self. In the poems he had spoken to the individual Muslim heart. At Allahabad he addressed the collective political future. He said: the interior work of khudi must find an exterior state in which it can be lived out. Poets who see clearly must, at some point, translate the vision into a political proposal. Iqbal made the proposal. Others carried it. But the proposal was his, and the country that came out of it is one of the largest Muslim countries in the world.
