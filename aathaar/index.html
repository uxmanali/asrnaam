Aathaar

The traces they left. Stories of the men and women whose lives still shape ours.

Aathaar (آثار) is the plural of aathaar: trace, footprint, monument, legacy, impact. It shares its Arabic root ا-ث-ر with the name of AsrNaam itself. If AsrNaam is the reading of the impact of your name, Aathaar is the reading of the impact of theirs.

47 heroes. Every story drawn from the classical Islamic historical sources.

Companions of the Prophet ﷺ and the founding generation

c. 573 - 634 CE

أبو بكر الصديق Abu Bakr al-Siddiq

First adult male to accept Islam. Sole companion of the Prophet in the cave of Thawr. First Rashidun caliph.
c. 584 - 644 CE

عمر بن الخطاب Umar ibn al-Khattab

The second Rashidun caliph. Set out with a drawn sword to kill the Prophet and returned that same day as a Muslim.
c. 576 - 656 CE

عثمان بن عفان Uthman ibn Affan

Married two daughters of the Prophet ﷺ in succession, earning the epithet Dhun-Nurayn. Purchased the well of Rumah with his own wealth and gave it to the Muslims. Compiled the standard mushaf of the Quran. Assassinated at 84 while reading it.
c. 601 - 661 CE

علي بن أبي طالب Ali ibn Abi Talib

Cousin and son-in-law of the Prophet. The first male child to embrace Islam. Slept in the Prophet's bed on the night of the Hijra.
c. 555 - 619 CE

خديجة بنت خويلد Khadijah bint Khuwaylid

A wealthy Meccan merchant who employed a young Muhammad ﷺ, proposed marriage to him, and became the first person to believe in his prophecy. She was 40 and he was 25. Their marriage lasted 25 years and during her lifetime he took no other wife.
c. 605 - 632 CE

فاطمة الزهراء Fatimah az-Zahra

Youngest daughter of the Prophet ﷺ and Khadijah, married to Ali ibn Abi Talib, mother of Hasan and Husayn. She is the origin of the Sayyid lineage. Died six months after her father, aged 27.
c. 613 - 678 CE

عائشة بنت أبي بكر Aisha bint Abi Bakr

The beloved of the Prophet ﷺ after Khadijah. Narrator of 2,210 hadith. Cleared of slander by Quranic revelation. Taught the Companions after the Prophet's death for 44 years. The Prophet said: 'Take half your religion from Humaira.'
c. 568 - 625 CE

حمزة بن عبد المطلب Hamza ibn Abdul-Muttalib

Uncle of the Prophet ﷺ and his foster-brother. Accepted Islam in a moment of fury after Abu Jahl insulted the Prophet. Killed by Wahshi's spear at Uhud. The Prophet said of him: 'the master of the martyrs.'
c. 590 - 629 CE

جعفر بن أبي طالب Ja'far ibn Abi Talib

Elder brother of Ali. Led the Muslim delegation to the Christian Negus of Abyssinia, who wept at his recitation of Surah Maryam and refused to hand the refugees over. At Mu'tah he took the banner after Zayd fell. Both arms were cut off. He held the banner with his chest until he was cut down.
c. 580 - 640 CE

بلال بن رباح Bilal ibn Rabah

An Ethiopian slave in Mecca tortured on the burning sands under a boulder for accepting Islam. He answered every blow with one word: 'Ahad, ahad', One, One. Abu Bakr bought his freedom. He became the Prophet's muezzin, calling the adhan for the first time from the roof of the Kaaba on the day of conquest.
d. c. 634 CE

نسيبة بنت كعب Nusaybah bint Ka'ab

Went to Uhud with a waterskin to give the wounded water. Took up a sword when the Muslim line broke and stood over the Prophet with her body as his shield.
c. 595 - 674 CE

سعد بن أبي وقاص Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas

One of the ten promised paradise. First man to shoot an arrow in defence of Islam. The Prophet ﷺ said to him: 'Shoot, Sa'd. May my father and mother be sacrificed for you.' Commanded the Muslim army at Qadisiyya that broke the Sasanian Empire.
c. 592 - 642 CE

خالد بن الوليد Khalid ibn al-Waleed

The undefeated general. Fought against the Muslims at Uhud, embraced Islam six years later, and never lost a single battle across more than a hundred engagements.
626 - 680 CE

الحسين بن علي Husayn ibn Ali

Grandson of the Prophet ﷺ, second son of Ali and Fatimah. Refused to pledge allegiance to the tyranny of Yazid. Marched with 72 companions to Kufa, was surrounded at Karbala, cut off from water for three days, and killed on the tenth of Muharram. His death defines Islamic conscience.
c. 575 - 645 CE

الخنساء Al-Khansa

One of the great poets of the Arabic language. Her elegies for her brothers Sakhr and Muawiya established the classical form of the marthiya. Accepted Islam in old age. Sent her four sons to Qadisiyya. When she heard all four had been killed, she said: 'Praise be to Allah who honoured me with their martyrdom.'

Generals, conquerors, and sultans

c. 585 - 664 CE

عمرو بن العاص Amr ibn al-As

A late Meccan convert to Islam who became one of the most gifted military commanders in Islamic history. Took Egypt from the Byzantines in the winter of 640-641 with an army of 4,000. Founded Fustat, the seed of modern Cairo. Governor of Egypt for over a decade.
d. c. 720 CE

طارق بن زياد Tariq ibn Ziyad

A Berber Muslim commander who crossed the strait from Africa to Iberia in 711 CE with seven thousand men. Won. Andalusia was Muslim for the next seven and a half centuries.
c. 640 - 716 CE

موسى بن نصير Musa ibn Nusayr

Umayyad governor of Ifriqiya (North Africa) who authorised the crossing to Iberia in 711. Sent Tariq ibn Ziyad with 7,000 men, then crossed himself with 18,000 to complete the conquest. Recalled to Damascus and died in disgrace, a warning about the politics of empire.
1118 - 1174 CE

نور الدين زنكي Nur ad-Din Zangi

Turkic sultan of Aleppo and Damascus. Unified the Sunni Muslim world of Syria for the first time in a generation, laid the foundation of the counter-Crusade, and prepared the political geography that Salahuddin would inherit and complete. Known for personal ascetic piety and merciless justice.
1137 - 1193 CE

صلاح الدين الأيوبي Salahuddin al-Ayyubi

The Kurdish sultan who recaptured Jerusalem for Islam eighty-eight years after the First Crusade. Died with nothing.
c. 1009 - 1106 CE

يوسف بن تاشفين Yusuf ibn Tashfin

Berber Almoravid ruler of the Maghreb and Al-Andalus. Founded Marrakech. Crossed the strait in 1086 at the desperate appeal of the Andalusi taifa kings and shattered Alfonso VI's army at Zallaqa. Extended Muslim rule in Iberia by 400 years.
1432 - 1481 CE

محمد الفاتح Muhammad al-Fatih

Ottoman sultan who took Constantinople in 1453 at the age of 21, fulfilling a hadith of the Prophet ﷺ: 'You shall conquer Constantinople. What a wonderful prince its prince, and what a wonderful army its army.' Ended the Byzantine Empire. Named Istanbul.
1494 - 1566 CE

سليمان القانوني Suleiman the Magnificent

Tenth Ottoman sultan. Ruled from 1520 to 1566, the longest reign in Ottoman history and the apex of the empire's power. Codified Ottoman civil law (kanun). Conquered Belgrade, Rhodes, Baghdad, most of Hungary. Died in the field at 71 during his last siege.
c. 1191 - 1281 CE

أرطغرل Ertugrul

Chief of the Kayı clan of the Oghuz Turks, granted a small frontier fief in Söğüt in western Anatolia by the Seljuk Sultan for defending the Byzantine frontier. His son Osman would build on that fief the Ottoman Empire, which would last 623 years.
1618 - 1707 CE

أورنغزيب عالمگير Aurangzeb Alamgir

Ruled the Mughal Empire for 49 years, the longest of any Mughal, from Kashmir to the Deccan and Bengal to Kabul, an empire of some 158 million subjects, then a quarter of the world's population. Personally austere, a hafiz of the Quran, controversial for his revocation of Akbar's syncretic tolerance policies.

Scholars, saints, and founders

c. 717 - 801 CE

رابعة العدوية Rabia al-Adawiyya

An eighth-century Iraqi mystic born in slavery and freed by a master who feared to keep her. Refused every marriage proposal to devote herself to Allah alone. Wrote what remain the founding poems of the Sufi 'theology of pure love', worship without hope of paradise, without fear of hellfire.
c. 800 - 880 CE

فاطمة الفهرية Fatima al-Fihriyah

A wealthy immigrant to Fez from Kairouan who used her inheritance to found the university of al-Qarawiyyin in 859 CE. Twelve centuries later it is still operating, listed by UNESCO as the oldest continuously-functioning degree-granting institution in the world.

Why we call it Aathaar

In classical Arabic ethics, al-aathaar al-baqiyah, "the enduring trace," is the technical term for what a person leaves behind that keeps affecting the world after they are gone. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said: "When a person dies, all their deeds come to an end except three: a running charity, useful knowledge left behind, or a righteous child who prays for them."

The people you read about here left aathaar. Their fingerprints are still on the world we walk on.
