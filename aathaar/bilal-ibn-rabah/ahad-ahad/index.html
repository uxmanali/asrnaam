Aathaar · Bilal ibn Rabah

Ahad, ahad, One, One

AsrNaam Editorial · From the classical sources

Umayya ibn Khalaf dragged Bilal onto the burning sands of Mecca at midday, laid a boulder on his chest, and demanded he renounce Islam. Bilal answered every blow with the same word: 'ahad, ahad', One, One. Abu Bakr passed by, saw the scene, and bought his freedom on the spot.

Bilal ibn Rabah was owned in Mecca by Umayya ibn Khalaf of the Banu Jumah, one of the wealthier clans of the city. His mother was Hamama, an Ethiopian slave who had been taken from her homeland. His father was Rabah, also a slave. Bilal was born in slavery around 580 CE and had never known any other life.

When the Prophet ﷺ began to preach in Mecca, most of the earliest converts were from the merchant class, from Quraysh, from the Meccan aristocracy. But a scatter of slaves also heard the message and believed. Bilal was among them.

Umayya, when he learned that Bilal had accepted Islam, was furious. Every possession of a Meccan aristocrat, in his eyes, was an extension of his own honour. To have a slave in his household who followed the religion of the Meccan aristocracy's chief enemy was an intolerable stain. He decided to publicly demonstrate his authority.

He dragged Bilal into the open street of Mecca at midday. The sand was the hottest surface in the city, hot enough that a hand pressed to it would blister in seconds. He forced Bilal to lie down on the sand. Then he ordered a great boulder brought and laid it on Bilal's chest. Bilal could not move. The sand burned his back. The boulder crushed his ribs. Umayya walked around him and demanded he renounce Islam and reaffirm the gods of Quraysh.

Bilal answered him with one word. He said: ahad. One.

Umayya struck him. Bilal said: ahad.

Umayya threatened him. Bilal said: ahad, ahad.

Umayya offered him wealth if he would recant. Umayya threatened him with death if he would not. Bilal said: ahad, ahad. One, One.

The classical sources record that this torture went on for several days. Boys of Mecca were reportedly brought to watch and to spit on Bilal. Umayya went home each night convinced the next day Bilal would break. Bilal, when he could still speak, said ahad.

One day Abu Bakr al-Siddiq passed through the street where Bilal was being tortured. He was a merchant. He was one of the earliest Muslims. He saw Bilal on the sand with the boulder on his chest and Umayya standing over him.

Abu Bakr walked up to Umayya and said: "O Umayya, have you no fear of Allah? How long will you go on with this poor man?" Umayya answered: "You are the one who ruined him. Save him from what you see." Abu Bakr said: "I will do that. I have a black slave stronger and more robust than him. He is of your religion. I will exchange him for this one." Umayya said: "I accept."

Abu Bakr made the exchange the same afternoon. He took Bilal home. He freed him on the spot without asking any service in return. Bilal became a free man.

The Prophet ﷺ, when the Muslim community moved to Medina and the salat was established as the pillar of the community's daily life, chose Bilal as the first muezzin of Islam. His voice was deep and resonant. He called the adhan five times a day from the mosque of the Prophet for the whole Medinan period.

On the day of the conquest of Mecca, 8 AH, the Prophet ﷺ ordered Bilal to climb to the roof of the Kaaba itself and call the adhan from atop the sanctuary. Bilal climbed the Kaaba and called: Allahu akbar, Allahu akbar. Ashhadu an la ilaha illa Allah. Ashhadu anna Muhammadan rasulullah. The men and women of Quraysh, some of whom had watched him tortured on the sand a few years earlier, heard him from below.

Umayya himself was already dead. He had been killed at Badr in 2 AH, six years before the conquest, by Bilal in single combat. Bilal, when he cornered Umayya on the battlefield of Badr, is said to have shouted the takbir and finished him with a spear. The sources record that Bilal's account of the moment was brief. He said only that Allah had brought the account to its right end.

Bilal outlived the Prophet ﷺ. After the Prophet's death he could no longer bring himself to give the adhan. He said his heart broke each time he began. He left Medina and settled in Syria as a soldier of the frontier. He gave the adhan only twice more: once when Umar visited Jerusalem, and once, according to a tradition, when he saw the Prophet in a dream. He died in Damascus about 640 CE.

What the story teaches

The story of Bilal on the sand is remembered by Muslims because it settles a question about tawhid at the level of the body. Bilal on the sand knew, in the way only a tortured man knows, what the word ahad meant. It was not a theological proposition. It was the single anchor that held him against the boulder on his chest and the burning sand under his back. He said ahad because ahad was the only thing he had. The Muslim tradition remembers Bilal as one of the greatest of the Companions because he understood, before the Islamic theology was even fully articulated in words, that tawhid is what a soul says when there is nothing left to say and nowhere left to go. It is the last word of every believer. Bilal, born a slave, is one of the great teachers of what that word costs.
