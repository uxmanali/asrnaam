Section 3 · Synthesis

What This Reading Says About You

Al-Battani joins fire to earth, the will that decides with the patience that lasts. This is a name that asks its bearer to burn steadily rather than in bursts, and to plant what the fire has cleared. Running through the reading is Al-Ahad, the One, carried as a singular, undivided presence, and beside it Al-Qayyum, the Self-Sustaining, adding a self-holding constancy. A person named Al-Battani plants what their fire has cleared.

Section 4 · What Families Say

What People Usually Mean by This Name

The nisba of Abu Abdullah Muhammad ibn Jabir al-Battani (d. 929 CE), Latinised as Albategnius, one of the greatest astronomers of the Islamic Golden Age. The nisba is from Battan, a place near Harran in upper Mesopotamia.

Al-Battani's Kitab az-Zij (Sabian Tables) revised Ptolemy's astronomical parameters with observations from the Raqqa observatory, giving improved values for the length of the solar year, the precession of the equinoxes, and the orbit of the Moon. Copernicus cited him in the De revolutionibus.

The name preserves the memory of a scholar whose observations were still shaping European astronomy seven centuries after his death.

Section 5 · Bearers

Who Has Carried This Name

  • Abū ʿAbd Allāh Muḥammad ibn Jābir ibn Sinān al-Battānī al-Ḥarrānī al-Ṣābiʾ (c. 858 – 929 CE · Harran / Raqqa). Al-Battānī's Kitāb al-Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ revised Ptolemy's planetary tables with new observations made at Raqqa over decades. He determined the inclination of the ecliptic and the length of the solar year with significantly improved precision, identified the motion of the solar apogee, and gave more accurate values for the precession of the equinoxes. His tables circulated as Tabulae Sabaeae in Latin Europe and were used by Copernicus, who cited him repeatedly.
