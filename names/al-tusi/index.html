Famous Bearer

The historical figure who carried the name Al-Tusi.

  • Naṣīr al-Dīn Abū Jaʿfar Muḥammad ibn Muḥammad ibn al-Ḥasan al-Ṭūsī (1201 – 1274 CE · Tus (Khurasan) / Maragha). Al-Ṭūsī founded and directed the Maragha Observatory under the Ilkhanid Hulagu, producing the Zīj-i Īlkhānī. His geometrical innovation, the 'Ṭūsī couple', two circles producing linear motion, became a standard tool of late-Islamic astronomy and reappeared in Copernicus's De Revolutionibus, by what route remains debated. He also wrote a major commentary on Euclid, the influential ethical treatise Akhlāq-i Nāṣirī, and contributions to Twelver Shīʿī theology.

What Al-Tusi reveals

Al-Ṭūsī opens with Ṭa (ط), the wheel of completion, apt for an astronomer whose geometry organised the heavens.

Legacy

What Al-Tusi left behind

The Maragha tradition that he inaugurated, geometric models that decoupled physical motion from Ptolemy's equant, was the most innovative current of pre-modern astronomy. Akhlāq-i Nāṣirī shaped Islamic ethical thought for centuries.

Scholarly assessment of a figure of this scale remains an active conversation; this entry summarises the dominant historical reading.
