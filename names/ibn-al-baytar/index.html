Section 3 · Synthesis

What This Reading Says About You

Ibn al-Baytar joins fire to earth, the will that decides with the patience that lasts. This is a name that asks its bearer to burn steadily rather than in bursts, and to plant what the fire has cleared. Running through the reading is Al-Ahad, the One, carried as a singular, undivided presence, and beside it Al-Rahman, the Merciful, adding mercy that keeps moving even when tested. A person named Ibn al-Baytar tends to build things that outlast them.

Section 5 · Bearers

Who Has Carried This Name

  • Ḍiyāʾ al-Dīn Abū Muḥammad ʿAbdullāh ibn Aḥmad al-Mālaqī Ibn al-Bayṭār (c. 1197–1248 CE · Málaga (al-Andalus) / Cairo / Damascus). Ibn al-Bayṭār's Kitāb al-Jāmiʿ li-Mufradāt al-Adwiya wa-l-Aghdhiya (Compendium of Simple Drugs and Foods) is the most comprehensive pharmacopoeia produced in the Islamic world. It describes 1,400 medicinal substances, 300 of them not previously recorded, drawn from his own field surveys across al-Andalus, North Africa, Anatolia, Syria, and Egypt. Each entry gives the plant in Arabic, Berber, Persian, Latin, and sometimes Spanish or Greek, with therapeutic uses and preparation.

Legacy

What Ibn al-Baytar left behind

The Jāmiʿ was translated into Latin and remained a standard reference in European medical schools until the seventeenth century. Its multilingual entries make it one of the most important sources for the history of Mediterranean ethnobotany. Modern botanical synonyms still trace through Ibn al-Bayṭār.

Scholarly assessment of a figure of this scale remains an active conversation; this entry summarises the dominant historical reading.
