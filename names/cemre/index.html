Dictionary Meaning

What Cemre means

Ember, glowing coal — from Turkish جمرة (cemre/jamra — ember, glowing coal, also the three stones thrown during Hajj). In Turkish tradition, Cemre refers to a beautiful meteorological folk belief: that there are three 'cemre' (warm masses) that fall in February — one into the air, one into the water, one into the earth — each warming the world a little more toward spring. The name is associated with this warming — the first warmth after winter.

The name Cemre has become extremely popular in Turkey in recent decades for its poetic quality: the ember that starts the spring, the warmth that persists through cold, the heat that comes from within. Jamarat (plural of jamra) are also the stones thrown at the symbolic devil during Hajj.

The Letters

Reading Cemre through Ilm ul Huroof

Every name is a sequence of principles. The first letter governs the outward self. The middle letters govern the interior life. The final letter governs how things resolve. For Persian, Turkish and Urdu names, the reading draws on the nearest Arabic letter equivalents.

Cemre opens with Jeem — comprehensive beauty. Then Meem — love, depth, sustaining. Then Ra — mercy in perpetual motion. Then Ha — divine breath, presence. Comprehensive beauty (Jeem) — love and depth (Meem) — mercy in motion (Ra) — divine presence (Ha). The ember: beautiful and comprehensive, fuelled by love and depth, moving with mercy, breathing divine presence. The ember breathes — it needs air to keep its glow.

The Portrait

What Cemre asks of its bearer

People named Cemre tend to have a quality of inner warmth that others feel, especially in cold or difficult moments. The Jeem gives them comprehensive beauty. The Meem gives them love and depth as the fuel of their warmth. The Ra gives them perpetual motion: their warmth keeps moving, keeps reaching. The Ha close breathes presence: their warmth is genuinely present, not just a quality talked about.

A Notable Bearer

In Turkish folk tradition, the three Cemre mark the warming of the world toward spring. Cemre names the woman who carries this warming quality — whose presence marks the turning from cold to warmth, from difficulty to ease.
