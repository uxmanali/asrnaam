Section 3 · Synthesis

What This Reading Says About You

Al-Farabi's letters draw from many elements at once, a full palette, not a single note. This is a name that asks its bearer to hold contradictions gracefully: warmth and steadiness, movement and depth. Running through the reading is Al-Ahad, the One, carried by a person as a singular, undivided presence. A person named Al-Farabi lives with more inside them than any one word can hold.

Section 4 · What Families Say

What People Usually Mean by This Name

The nisba of Abu Nasr Muhammad al-Farabi (d. 950 CE), the second teacher (al-Muallim al-Thani, second only to Aristotle in the classical Islamic reckoning). From Farab, the city on the Syr Darya in Khurasan (in what is now southern Kazakhstan).

Al-Farabi wrote foundational works on political philosophy (al-Madina al-Fadila, The Virtuous City), on logic, on music theory (Kitab al-Musiqa al-Kabir), and on the harmony of the philosophies of Plato and Aristotle. He was the teacher of the tradition that Ibn Sina and Ibn Rushd would inherit.

The name preserves the memory of one of the founders of Islamic philosophical tradition.

Section 5 · Bearers

Who Has Carried This Name

  • Abū Naṣr Muḥammad ibn Muḥammad al-Fārābī (c. 872–950 CE · Fārāb (Khurasan) / Baghdad / Damascus). Known to later philosophers as al-Muʿallim al-Thānī, the Second Master, after Aristotle himself. Al-Farabi systematised the philosophy of his predecessors and built a coherent metaphysics, political theory, and theory of language. His Madina al-Fāḍila (The Virtuous City) is the first major work of Islamic political philosophy. His Kitāb al-Mūsīqā al-Kabīr remains a foundational text in the history of music theory.

Legacy

What Al-Farabi left behind

Ibn Sina credits Al-Farabi for opening Aristotle's Metaphysics to him after forty failed readings. Maimonides considered Al-Farabi's logical works second only to Aristotle's. His vision of the philosopher-prophet, the leader whose insight is both rational and revealed, runs through the entire later tradition.

Scholarly assessment of a figure of this scale remains an active conversation; this entry summarises the dominant historical reading.
