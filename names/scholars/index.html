Famous Islamic Scholars of the Golden Age

The astronomers, physicians, philosophers, geographers, engineers, and jurists of the Islamic Golden Age (8th-15th c. CE), from al-Khwārizmī to Ibn Khaldūn, each with their classical dates, region, and field.

41 entries

From the eighth through the fifteenth century CE, the lands ruled by Muslim caliphates and sultanates were home to what historians call the Islamic Golden Age. A sustained civilizational moment when Arabic was the language of science and scholarship for the entire region from al-Andalus (Iberia) through North Africa, the Levant, Mesopotamia, Persia, and Central Asia. Patronage came from caliphal libraries like the Bayt al-Ḥikma in ʿAbbāsid Baghdad, the Umayyad library of Córdoba, the Niẓāmiyya madrasas of the Seljuk world, the Maragha observatory of the Ilkhanids, and the courts of Samarqand and Cairo.

The scholars below worked on the questions that defined the science and culture of their world. The structure of the cosmos, the workings of the body, the foundations of law, the nature of being, the order of history. Their works were translated into Latin, Hebrew, and Persian, and they shaped European intellectual life for the next five centuries. On AsrNaam, each has a canonical page with the Arabic name, Urdu transliteration, classical dates, region, field, and a famous-bearer summary drawing on Ibn Khallikan's Wafayāt al-Aʿyān and the Encyclopaedia of Islam.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Islamic Golden Age?

The Islamic Golden Age (c. 8th-15th centuries CE) is the period when Arabic was the language of science and scholarship across the medieval Muslim world. Caliphal patronage at the Bayt al-Ḥikma in Baghdad, the Umayyad library in Córdoba, the Niẓāmiyya madrasas, and observatories from Maragha to Samarqand supported a wave of scholarship in mathematics, astronomy, medicine, philosophy, geography, and law whose results passed into Latin Europe and shaped the Renaissance.

Did Muslim scholars invent algebra?

Al-Khwārizmī systematised algebra in his Kitāb al-Jabr wa-l-Muqābala (c. 820 CE). That's where the word "algebra" comes from. He did not invent the field. Babylonian, Egyptian, and Indian mathematicians had worked on quadratic problems for centuries before. But he organised it into a coherent science with a method that could be taught and extended. His name, Latinised as Algoritmi, became the English word "algorithm".

Who was the most important Muslim philosopher?

The candidates are Ibn Sīnā (Avicenna, d. 1037), al-Ghazālī (d. 1111), and Ibn Rushd (Averroes, d. 1198). Each pages on AsrNaam covers their life, works, and ranking in the philosophical tradition. Ibn Sīnā is the central figure of falsafa; al-Ghazālī is the central figure of theological orthodoxy and Sufism; Ibn Rushd is the figure whose Aristotle commentaries shaped Latin scholasticism.

Who really discovered pulmonary circulation?

Ibn al-Nafīs (d. 1288) described the pulmonary circuit in his Sharḥ Tashrīḥ al-Qānūn. That blood passes from the right ventricle through the lungs to the left ventricle, against Galen's account of a porous interventricular septum. This is roughly three centuries before Servetus (1553) and Harvey (1628). The priority is now settled in the history of medicine.
