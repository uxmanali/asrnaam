Famous Bearer

The historical figure who carried the name Ibn Battuta.

  • Abū ʿAbdullāh Muḥammad ibn ʿAbdullāh ibn Baṭṭūṭa al-Lawātī al-Ṭanjī (1304–1369 CE · Tangier (Maghreb) / Delhi / Mali). Ibn Battuta set out from Tangier in 1325 at the age of twenty-one for the hajj and did not return for twenty-nine years. His Riḥla (Journey) records travels across the Maghreb, Egypt, Arabia, the Levant, Anatolia, the Eurasian steppe, Central Asia, the Indian subcontinent, where he served as a qadi under the Delhi Sultanate, the Maldives, Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, China, sub-Saharan Mali, and Iberia. Total distance: approximately 120,000 kilometres, three times Marco Polo's range.

Legacy

What Ibn Battuta left behind

The Riḥla is one of the most important geographical and ethnographic documents to survive the medieval world. Its account of Mali under Mansa Musa, of Ceylon, and of Yuan China are among the few non-Chinese sources for those societies in the fourteenth century. Modern UNESCO heritage scholarship still consults it.

Scholarly assessment of a figure of this scale remains an active conversation; this entry summarises the dominant historical reading.
