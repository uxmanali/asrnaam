Dictionary Meaning

What Iman means

Īmān (إيمان) comes from the Arabic root أ-م-ن — the same root as Amīn (trustworthy), amāna (a trust held in keeping), and aman (safety). Where the English 'faith' often connotes belief in propositions, the Arabic īmān is closer to deep trust — the conviction that what God has said is true, the inner peace that comes from resting one's weight on it. The Qurʾān defines the believer (muʾmin) by this quality: 'Truly the believers are those whose hearts tremble when Allah is mentioned' (8:2).

As a name, Iman has been used as both masculine and (more commonly today) feminine. The most internationally recognised bearer is Iman Abdulmajid, the Somali-American supermodel and businesswoman, the wife of David Bowie. The name is widespread across the entire Muslim world — Egypt, the Levant, the Gulf, the Maghreb, Pakistan, Indonesia, Malaysia, the Horn of Africa. Notable other bearers include the Tunisian painter Iman Smaoui and the Saudi Arabian football manager Iman al-Husseini. The compound form ʿAbd al-Mūʾmin ('servant of the Believer') is a Name-of-Allah masculine form.

How This Shows Up in Life

Personality & relational pattern

People named Iman tend to be open and self-possessed in how they meet the world. The opening Alif carries the pattern inward — a strong sense of self and a tendency to begin things, and someone who draws others toward them as a starting point. The closing Nun suggests a person who ends inwardly, contemplatively.

An interpretive reading drawn from the letters of the name — for reflection, not prediction.

Reading Iman through Ilm ul Huroof

Every Arabic name is a sequence of ontological principles. The first letter governs the outward self. The middle letters govern the interior. The final letter governs how things resolve.

The Portrait

What Iman asks of its bearer

The name opens with the long Alif (إ), travels through Mīm (م), the letter of love, and closes through Nūn (ن), the letter of inwardness. A bearer of Iman is named into the most foundational quality of the Muslim life: the inner trust that allows a person to act under uncertainty.
